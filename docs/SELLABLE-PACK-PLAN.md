# What it would take to make one pack genuinely sellable

Scope for turning the **Reconstruction Starter Pack** into a product you could ethically and safely sell. Do this for one pack first; don't build a "library" until one pack proves people buy.

## Reality check (so effort matches payoff)
- **Market:** real but crowded. Teachers Pay Teachers (TpT), your own site (Gumroad/Stripe), and homeschool buyers.
- **Pricing:** a single well-made unit typically sells for **$6–$12**; a bundle (unit + modified version + answer keys + assessment) **$12–$25**. Free sample lesson drives the paid conversion.
- **Honest expectation:** most sellers earn a little; a few earn a lot, driven mostly by marketing and niche fit, not by how good the materials are. Treat early sales as a *demand test*, not income.
- **The real bottleneck is your review time**, not code. Everything below is authoring/verification, which is exactly why a "library" is expensive to scale.

## The non-negotiable review checklist (before a dollar changes hands)
You are the author of record. AI drafted and fact-checked this, but selling unreviewed AI output is a reputational and accuracy risk. Verify, unit by unit:
- [ ] **Every primary-source excerpt** against the original text (wording, and that it's genuinely public domain). Sources are the thing a knowledgeable buyer will check first; one wrong "quote" sinks the product.
- [ ] **Every attribution** (author, title, date, "public domain").
- [ ] **All dates, names, statutes, and court cases** in the readings, quiz, and DBQ.
- [ ] **The quiz answer key** — confirm each answer and explanation.
- [ ] **The DBQ documents and rubric** — usable and accurate.
- [ ] **The modified reading** — actually simpler, still accurate.
- [ ] **Sensitivity framing** for teaching slavery/Reconstruction reads the way you'd want in *your* building.
- [ ] **The standards note stays** — sample thematic categories only, "verify against your course of study."

## Gaps to close for a paid product
The demo has the teaching content; a *product* needs the wrapper:
1. **Editable + printable formats.** Buyers expect a PDF *and* an editable Google Doc / Word version they can tweak. (The app's Copy/Download gives Markdown as a starting point.)
2. **A clean answer-key / teacher edition** separated from the student packet.
3. **A title/cover page and a preview** (first lesson + a few thumbnails) — the free sample that sells the rest.
4. **A one-page standards-alignment sheet** the buyer can hand up the chain (the app's `/print/[slug]` one-pager is the model).
5. **Terms of use / license** — single-teacher vs. building vs. district; "not for resale or AI training." A one-paragraph license on the title page is standard on TpT.
6. **Your name/brand and contact** on every page.
7. **Accessibility pass** — readable fonts, alt text for any images, print tested on Letter and A4.

## Packaging & listing (once reviewed)
- **Format:** a zipped folder — Student Packet (PDF), Modified Version (PDF), Teacher Edition/Keys (PDF), editable Docs, and the one-page alignment sheet.
- **List on:** TpT (reach) + a Gumroad or Stripe payment link from your own site (margin, brand). Same product, two SKUs.
- **Price:** start ~$8–$12 for the pack; offer the Day 1 "Freedom on Paper" lesson **free** as the wedge.
- **Describe it around the method,** not the topic: "not just is-it-biased, but what-does-the-bias-do," verified sources, built-in differentiation. That's the part a chatbot can't match.

## Rough effort
- Careful review of one 5-day unit + building the product wrapper: **~8–15 focused hours.**
- Listing + a free sample + a simple sales page: **~2–4 hours.**
- Then *stop and measure* before making pack #2.

## Legal / ethical guardrails
- You must be able to stand behind every factual claim and source — verify, don't trust.
- Keep everything **public-domain**; the Civil Rights material deliberately avoids copyrighted King texts and photographs for this reason.
- Never put student data into any of this; it collects none by design.
