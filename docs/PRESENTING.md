## Cover-letter / application paragraph
> I believe social studies matters *more* in the AI age, not less: when anyone can generate a confident-sounding essay in seconds, the scarce skill is judgment — evaluating evidence, bias, and consequence. To make that concrete, I built History Judgment Lab, a source-based history curriculum spanning Medieval Europe to American Government. Every unit is anchored in genuine primary sources and a single through-line — not just "is this biased?" but "what does the bias *do*?" It also demonstrates how I'd use AI in a classroom responsibly: as a drafting and fact-checking assistant, never as the authority, with materials reviewed before they reach students.

## Interview walkthrough (a tight 2–3 minute demo)
Open the live URL and move in this order — each stop makes one point:

1. **Landing (`/`)** — "The value in ten seconds." Read the thesis: information was democratized by the internet, execution by AI, but **judgment has to be taught**. *Point:* I can frame why this subject matters now.
2. **Scope & Sequence (`/scope-and-sequence`)** — "This is a system, not a folder of worksheets." Show the five-strand spine, the unit sequence, and the documents that travel between units. *Point:* coherent, vertical curriculum design.
3. **Reconstruction (`/reconstruction`)** — the depth. Driving question, real primary sources, the 5-day arc, the DBQ and rubric, and the differentiation supports. Flip the **Reading-level toggle** (Full / Modified / EL). *Point:* rigorous and accessible to every learner.
4. **From Bias Detection to Civic Judgment (`/civic-judgment`)** — the differentiator. The Bias Consequence Ladder and "what does the bias do?" *Point:* this teaches transferable civic reasoning, not just content.
5. **Judgment Lab (`/judgment-lab`)** — AI literacy done soberly (run the ladder on an AI summary; algorithmic feeds; your own reasoning). *Point:* I engage AI honestly, without hype or panic.
6. **Administrator one-pager (`/print/reconstruction`)** — click Print. *Point:* I think about the people who have to approve and defend what I teach.

Close with the Responsible AI page's line: *"AI does not remove the need for judgment. It raises the cost of not having it."*

## Anticipated questions (and honest, strong answers)
- **"Did AI write this?"** — "Yes, I used AI to draft and adversarially fact-check content at scale — that's the point. What I did *not* do is let it decide what's true or what a lesson means. Every source, framing, and instructional choice is mine, and nothing goes to students without human review. That's exactly the stance I'd model for kids: AI as an assistant, judgment stays with the person." (Then verify a couple of sources live to show you know the material.)
- **"Is it standards-aligned?"** — "It shows sample thematic alignment categories on purpose; the app explicitly says to verify exact codes against the current course of study before classroom use. I'd map it to *your* framework rather than assert codes I can't guarantee."
- **"How is this different from a chatbot lesson generator?"** — "A generator gives you one disposable lesson. This is a coherent *method* — the same judgment framework spiraling across six units with verified sources and built-in differentiation. That's the part AI can't hand you on demand."
